Borneo Elephant

Scientific name: Elephas maximus borneensis

Conservation Status: Endangered

Estimated Population: fewer than 1,500

Avg. Lifespan: 55-70 years

The Bornean Elephant, also known as the Borneo Pygmy Elephant is a subspecies of the Asian elephant that inhabits the forests of Borneo, primarily in Sabah, Malaysia. Recognized for its smaller size, rounder face, straighter tusks, and longer tail compared to its mainland relatives, this elephant is among the most genetically distinct populations of Asian elephants. Its gentle temperament and unique characteristics make it an iconic yet vulnerable species of the region.

Over the past few decades, extensive deforestation, agricultural expansion, and human settlement have significantly reduced its natural habitat. As forests shrink and fragment, elephants increasingly venture into plantations and rural areas in search of food, leading to rising human-elephant conflict. Many elephants suffer injuries from illegal snares or retaliatory actions, while the loss of continuous forest blocks isolates herds and increases inbreeding risks. In the last 40 years, Sabah alone has lost about 60% of suitable elephant habitat due to logging and the spread of oil palm plantations.

Conservation efforts are ongoing through collaborations between the Sabah Wildlife Department, WWF-Malaysia, and other partners. These programs aim to restore wildlife corridors, improve habitat connectivity, and reduce human-wildlife conflict through community engagement and awareness. The Bornean Elephant serves as both a keystone and flagship species for Borneo’s rainforests — its survival is deeply linked to the overall health of the island’s ecosystem.

Quick Facts

Names

General Name: Borneo Elephant // Borneo Pygmy Elephant
Scientific name: Elephas maximus borneensis

Diet

Herbivorous: grasses, leaves, bark, bamboo shoots & tropical fruits

Lifespan

~55-70 years depending on survival conditions

Habitat

Tropical rainforests, river valleys, & lowland forests of Sabah

Behaviour

Social & Matriarchal; herds are eld by older females and often travel along forect corridors in search of food & water

Population

Estimated to be fewer than 1,500 scattered into several isolated groups across Sabah

Threats

Habitat loss, hunting, fragmentation, illegal snares & agricultural expansion

Conservation Efforts

Habitat restoration, wildlife corridors & anti-poaching patrols by WWF & Sabah Wildlife Department
