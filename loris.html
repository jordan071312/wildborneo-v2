Bornean Slow Loris

Scientific name: Nycticebus borneanus

Conservation Status: Vulnerable

Estimated Population: unknown

Avg. Lifespan: ~20 years

The Bornean Slow Loris is a nocturnal primate native to the island of Borneo, particularly found in the forests of Sabah, Indonesia, and parts of the Philippines. Known for its large, forward-facing eyes and slow, careful movements, it spends most of its life in trees, navigating the forest canopy with stealth and precision. Its arboreal lifestyle and nocturnal habits make it a rarely seen but ecologically vital species in the tropical ecosystem.

As an omnivore, the Bornean Slow Loris feeds on fruits, nectar, insects, and small animals. Its feeding habits play an important ecological role — by pollinating flowers and dispersing seeds, it contributes to forest regeneration and the maintenance of biodiversity. Despite its small size, the loris is an essential part of its ecosystem, helping to keep insect populations balanced and supporting healthy forest growth.

Unfortunately, this species faces growing threats from human activities. Deforestation caused by logging, agriculture, and expanding settlements has led to severe habitat loss. In addition, the Bornean Slow Loris is targeted for the illegal pet trade and tourist attractions, where it is often captured and mistreated. Forest fires also threaten its survival, further shrinking its already fragmented habitats and pushing it closer to endangerment.
