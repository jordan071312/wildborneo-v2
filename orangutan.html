Bornean Orangutan

Scientific name: Pongo pygmaeus

Conservation Status: Critically Endangered

Estimated Population: ~104,700

Avg. Lifespan: ~35-60 years

The Bornean Orangutan is one of the most iconic species of Southeast Asia’s rainforests, found mainly in the lowland, swamp, and mountainous forests of Borneo. Known for their intelligence and distinct reddish-brown fur, these great apes spend most of their lives in trees, where they build nests for sleeping and move gracefully through the canopy. They play an essential ecological role as seed dispersers, contributing to the health and regeneration of tropical forests.

Orangutans are primarily frugivorous, with fruits making up the majority of their diet, though they also consume bark, leaves, flowers, bird eggs, sap, and insects. Their feeding habits not only sustain them but also help maintain forest biodiversity by spreading seeds throughout their habitats. These solitary and gentle primates rely heavily on vast, undisturbed forests to survive, making them highly vulnerable to human encroachment.

Unfortunately, the Bornean Orangutan faces severe threats from habitat loss and fragmentation due to logging, mining, and agricultural expansion — particularly palm oil plantations. Deforestation and forest fires have destroyed large areas of their habitat, while illegal hunting and wildlife trade have further diminished their populations. Conservation initiatives focus on habitat restoration, anti-poaching enforcement, and rehabilitation programs for rescued individuals. Through cooperation among governments, NGOs, and local communities, efforts continue to protect remaining populations and ensure their survival for generations to come.
