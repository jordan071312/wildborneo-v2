Sunda Pangolin

Scientific name: Manis javanica

Conservation Status: Critically Endangered

Estimated Population: ~50,000

Avg. Lifespan: unknown

The Sunda Pangolin is a nocturnal mammal native to Southeast Asia, including countries such as India, China, and parts of Indonesia and Malaysia. It inhabits forests, grasslands, and savannas, with some populations being arboreal while others are primarily terrestrial. Known for its distinctive protective scales, the pangolin is an elusive species that plays an important role in maintaining ecological balance through its insectivorous diet.

This species feeds almost exclusively on ants and termites, consuming thousands of insects daily. Its specialized long tongue allows it to extract insects from narrow crevices, while its digging behavior aerates the soil and helps control insect populations. By doing so, the pangolin contributes to soil health and forest ecosystem functioning, demonstrating its ecological importance despite its small size and solitary habits.

Unfortunately, the Sunda Pangolin faces critical threats from human activity. It is the most trafficked mammal in the world, with its scales highly valued in traditional medicine and its meat considered a luxury delicacy in some cultures. Habitat loss from deforestation due to agriculture, logging, and urban development further endangers the species, making it increasingly difficult to find both shelter and food. Despite international trade bans, illegal poaching continues to drive its population decline.
