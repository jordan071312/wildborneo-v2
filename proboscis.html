Proboscis Monkey

Scientific name: Nasalis larvatus

Conservation Status: Endangered

Estimated Population: ~16,500

Avg. Lifespan: ~20 years

The Proboscis Monkey is a distinctive primate native to the rainforests of Borneo, particularly in mangroves, riverine forests, and lowland areas of Sabah and Kalimantan. Known for its large, protruding nose and pot-bellied appearance, it is an agile arboreal species that spends much of its life in trees and along riverbanks. Its unique adaptations allow it to thrive in wetland forests, where it plays an important ecological role by dispersing seeds and maintaining the health of forest ecosystems.

This species is primarily folivorous, feeding on leaves, seeds, and unripe fruits, though it occasionally consumes insects. Proboscis Monkeys have complex, chambered stomachs that rely on symbiotic bacteria to break down tough plant material, enabling them to extract nutrients efficiently. Their feeding habits not only support their own survival but also help with forest regeneration through seed dispersal and maintaining plant diversity.

The Proboscis Monkey faces serious threats from human activity. Rampant deforestation for timber, settlement, and oil palm plantations has fragmented their habitat, forcing them to descend to the ground more frequently and travel longer distances to find food. This increased exposure puts them at risk from predators and human hunting, as some local communities consider them a delicacy. Conservation efforts are urgently needed to protect remaining populations and preserve their critical wetland habitats.

Quick Facts

Names
