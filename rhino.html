Bornean Rhinoceros

Scientific name: Dicerorhinus sumatrensis harrissoni

Conservation Status: Critically Endangered

Estimated Population: fewer than 80 (Indonesia)

This species is endemically extinct in Malaysia

Avg. Lifespan: ~35-40 years

The Bornean Rhinoceros is a critically endangered herbivore native to Southeast Asia, with its remaining populations confined primarily to Sumatra (Indonesia) and a few isolated areas in Borneo. Historically widespread across the region, these dense tropical rainforest dwellers are now nearly extinct in the wild. They are mostly solitary, except during mating or when mothers bond with calves, and are known for their nocturnal and crepuscular activity patterns, navigating hilly and mountainous forest areas under the cover of night.

Bornean Rhinoceroses are herbivores, feeding on a diverse array of leaves, twigs, bark, fruits, and over a hundred plant species. Their foraging plays an essential role in seed dispersal, supporting forest regeneration and maintaining ecosystem health. These rhinos are also excellent swimmers and wallowers, using mud baths to cool down and protect their skin from parasites, demonstrating both behavioral adaptability and ecological importance in their forest habitats.

The species faces critical threats from habitat loss and fragmentation, largely due to logging, palm oil plantations, and infrastructure development. With fewer than 80 individuals remaining, the population is extremely small, geographically isolated, and genetically fragmented, increasing risks of inbreeding and reproductive disorders. Although poaching has historically targeted rhino horns for traditional medicine and status symbols, strict protection laws have reduced hunting, but the long-term population damage has already made recovery difficult. Urgent conservation efforts are required to prevent the complete extinction of this iconic species.
